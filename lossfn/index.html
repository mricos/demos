Why Norms Matter for Regularization
$$\|x\|_p = \left(\sum_i |x_i|^p\right)^{1/p}$$

L1 (Lasso): Produces sparse weights — drives coefficients to exactly zero. The diamond-shaped constraint makes solutions "snap" to axes.

L2 (Ridge): Shrinks all weights uniformly toward zero. The circular constraint distributes penalty smoothly.

L∞ (Max-norm): Penalizes only the largest component. Rarely used alone but important in adversarial robustness — adversarial perturbations are bounded in L∞.

Why Logits? Why $\log$?
$$\text{softmax}(z_i) = \frac{e^{z_i / \tau}}{\sum_j e^{z_j / \tau}}$$

A neural network's final layer outputs logits $z$ — unbounded real numbers. We need probabilities (positive, sum to 1). The softmax function accomplishes this via exponentiation (makes positive) and normalization (makes sum to 1).

Why take the log in cross-entropy? The loss $-\log p(\text{true class})$ has crucial properties:
• When $p \to 1$ (correct, confident): $-\log(1) = 0$ — no penalty
• When $p \to 0$ (wrong, confident): $-\log(0) \to \infty$ — catastrophic penalty
• This is the negative log-likelihood — maximizing likelihood = minimizing cross-entropy

In transformers: each position predicts the next token via logits over the vocabulary. Cross-entropy loss between predicted distribution and one-hot target drives training. The $\log$ in cross-entropy cancels the $\exp$ in softmax, giving numerically stable gradients:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_i} = p_i - \mathbb{1}[i = y_{\text{true}}]$$

The gradient is simply "predicted minus actual" — elegant, bounded, and stable. This is why we work in log-space.

Context

Bias–Variance Tradeoff

$$\text{MSE} = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \sigma^2$$

The expected prediction error decomposes into three terms:

  • Bias² — error from wrong assumptions (underfitting). High for simple models.
  • Variance — sensitivity to training data (overfitting). High for complex models.
  • Irreducible noise $\sigma^2$ — inherent randomness in data.

The sweet spot minimizes total error. Regularization shifts this sweet spot rightward, allowing more complex models without overfitting.

Lp Norms

$$\|x\|_p = \left(\sum_i |x_i|^p\right)^{1/p}$$
  • $p=1$: Manhattan distance $|x_1|+|x_2|$
  • $p=2$: Euclidean distance $\sqrt{x_1^2+x_2^2}$
  • $p\to\infty$: $\max(|x_1|,|x_2|)$

Cross-Entropy

$$H(p,q) = -\sum_x p(x)\log q(x)$$

Measures how well distribution $q$ (model) approximates $p$ (truth). For classification with one-hot $p$, simplifies to $-\log q(y_{\text{true}})$.

EEG Frequency Bands

  • Delta (δ) 0.5–4 Hz: Deep sleep
  • Theta (θ) 4–8 Hz: Drowsiness, meditation
  • Alpha (α) 8–13 Hz: Relaxed wakefulness
  • Beta (β) 13–30 Hz: Active thinking, focus
  • Gamma (γ) 30–100 Hz: High-level processing

FSK encoding: Each disposition shifts dominant power to its characteristic band. A classifier must learn which spectral features distinguish states.
