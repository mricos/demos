The Riemannian Structure of Wasserstein Space

Otto calculus, gradient flows, displacement convexity, and synthetic curvature

Prerequisites: Basic differential geometry (tangent spaces, metrics), measure theory, and familiarity with optimal transport

Notation

1. Riemannian Manifolds: Quick Review

A Riemannian manifold (M, g) is a smooth manifold equipped with a smoothly-varying inner product gp : TpM × TpM → ℝ on each tangent space. This structure enables us to measure lengths, angles, and volumes intrinsically.

Definition (Riemannian Metric)

In local coordinates, the metric is specified by a positive-definite matrix gij(x), and the squared length element is:

$ds^2 = \textcolor{#14b8a6}{g}_{ij}(x) \, dx^i \, dx^j$

Geodesics are curves γ(t) that locally minimize arc length. They satisfy the geodesic equation:

Geodesic Equation
$$\ddot{\gamma}^k + \Gamma^k_{ij} \dot{\gamma}^i \dot{\gamma}^j = 0$$

where Γkij are the Christoffel symbols encoding how the coordinate frame rotates along the manifold.

Curvature measures the failure of parallel transport to be path-independent. The Ricci curvature Ric(v, v) measures how the volume of a small ball grows as you move in direction v:

  • Ric > 0: Volumes grow slower than in flat space (geodesics converge)
  • Ric = 0: Flat, Euclidean-like behavior
  • Ric < 0: Volumes grow faster (geodesics diverge)

2. The Space of Probability Measures

Let M be a Riemannian manifold. Define P₂(M) as the space of Borel probability measures μ on M with finite second moment: ∫ d(x, x₀)² dμ(x) < ∞ for some (hence all) x₀ ∈ M.

Definition (Wasserstein-2 Distance)

For μ, ν ∈ P₂(M), the Wasserstein-2 distance is:

$\textcolor{#a855f7}{W_2}(\mu, \nu)^2 = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu,\nu)} \int_{M \times M} d(x,y)^2 \, d\gamma(x,y)$

where Π(μ,ν) is the set of couplings with marginals μ and ν.

The space (P₂(M), W₂) is a complete metric space. But Otto's key observation is that it has much more structure: it is (formally) an infinite-dimensional Riemannian manifold.

3. Otto Calculus: The Riemannian Structure

Consider the subset P₂ac(M) of absolutely continuous measures, which we can identify with their densities ρ. What is the "tangent space" at ρ?

A tangent vector represents an infinitesimal perturbation of ρ. The continuity equation constrains how densities can change:

Continuity Equation
$$\partial_{\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}} \textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho} + \nabla \cdot (\textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho} \textcolor{#ec4899}{v}) = 0$$

A velocity field v generates a flow that transports mass. Otto identified the tangent space with gradient vector fields:

Definition (Otto's Tangent Space)

The tangent space at ρ is TρP₂ = {∇φ : φ ∈ C∞(M)}, with inner product:

$\langle \nabla \textcolor{#8b5cf6}{\phi}_1, \nabla \textcolor{#8b5cf6}{\phi}_2 \rangle_{\textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho}} = \int_M \langle \nabla \textcolor{#8b5cf6}{\phi}_1, \nabla \textcolor{#8b5cf6}{\phi}_2 \rangle_{\textcolor{#14b8a6}{g}} \, d\textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho}$

This inner product has a beautiful interpretation: it measures the kinetic energy of the velocity field v = ∇φ, weighted by the mass distribution ρ.

Theorem (Benamou-Brenier)

The Wasserstein distance equals the geodesic distance in Otto's metric:

$\textcolor{#a855f7}{W_2}(\mu_0, \mu_1)^2 = \inf_{\rho, v} \int_0^1 \int_M |\textcolor{#ec4899}{v}|^2 \textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho} \, dx \, dt$

subject to the continuity equation with ρ(0) = μ₀, ρ(1) = μ₁.

The infimum is achieved by the optimal transport velocity field, and the minimizing path is the W₂ geodesic!

4. Geodesics: McCann's Displacement Interpolation

On ℝⁿ, if T is the optimal transport map from μ₀ to μ₁ (given by Brenier's theorem as T = ∇φ for convex φ), the geodesic has a simple form:

McCann Interpolation
$$\mu_{\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}} = \left[(1-\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t})\text{Id} + \textcolor{#fbbf24}{t} T\right]_\# \mu_0$$

Each particle at position x moves along a straight line to T(x). At time t, it's at position (1−t)x + tT(x). The entire distribution "displaces" along these linear paths.

Important Distinction

Displacement interpolation (μt = Tt#μ₀) moves mass along geodesics.

Linear interpolation ((1−t)ρ₀ + tρ₁) blends density values pointwise.

These are fundamentally different! Displacement interpolation is the geodesic in W₂; linear interpolation is generally not.

On curved manifolds M, the formula generalizes using exponential maps: particles move along geodesics of M, not straight lines.

5. Gradient Flows in Wasserstein Space

Given a functional F : P₂(M) → ℝ, its gradient flow is the curve ρ(t) that descends F as steeply as possible in the W₂ metric:

Wasserstein Gradient Flow
$$\partial_{\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}} \textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho} = -\nabla_{\textcolor{#a855f7}{W}} F(\textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho})$$

The Wasserstein gradient ∇WF is defined via the first variation: for any test velocity v = ∇φ,

$\langle \nabla_W F, \nabla\phi \rangle_\rho = \frac{d}{d\epsilon}\Big|_{\epsilon=0} F(\rho_\epsilon)$

where ρε is the pushforward of ρ by the flow of v.

Theorem (Otto 2001)

Many classical PDEs are gradient flows of natural functionals:

Heat equation ∂tρ = Δρ F = Entropy
Fokker-Planck ∂tρ = Δρ + ∇·(ρ∇V) F = Free energy
Porous medium ∂tρ = Δ(ρm) F = Rényi entropy
Aggregation ∂tρ = ∇·(ρ∇W*ρ) F = Interaction

The entropy functional is particularly important:

Boltzmann Entropy
$$\textcolor{#f97316}{H}(\textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho}) = \int_M \textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho}(x) \log \textcolor{#3b82f6}{\rho}(x) \, dx$$

Its Wasserstein gradient is ∇WH = ∇(log ρ + 1) = ∇log ρ. The gradient flow ∂tρ = −∇·(ρ∇log ρ) = Δρ is exactly the heat equation!

Connection to Score-Based Diffusion

The score function s(x) = ∇log ρ(x) is precisely the Wasserstein gradient of entropy! Score-based generative models perform Wasserstein gradient ascent on log-likelihood.

6. Displacement Convexity

A functional F : P₂(M) → ℝ is displacement convex (or geodesically convex) if for any W₂ geodesic μt:

Displacement Convexity
$$F(\mu_{\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}}) \leq (1-\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}) F(\mu_0) + \textcolor{#fbbf24}{t} F(\mu_1)$$
Theorem (McCann 1997)

On ℝⁿ, the entropy H(ρ) = ∫ρ log ρ is displacement convex.

On curved manifolds, convexity of entropy depends on curvature. This is where the Lott-Sturm-Villani theory enters.

7. Synthetic Ricci Curvature: The CD(K,N) Condition

The breakthrough insight of Lott-Villani (2009) and Sturm (2006): Ricci curvature bounds can be characterized by convexity of entropy along W₂ geodesics.

Definition (CD(K,∞) Condition)

A metric measure space (X, d, m) satisfies CD(K, ∞) if for any two measures μ₀, μ₁ ∈ P₂(X), there exists a W₂ geodesic μt such that:

$\textcolor{#f97316}{H}(\mu_{\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}}) \leq (1-\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}) \textcolor{#f97316}{H}(\mu_0) + \textcolor{#fbbf24}{t} \textcolor{#f97316}{H}(\mu_1) - \frac{\textcolor{#22c55e}{K}}{2}\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}(1-\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t})\textcolor{#a855f7}{W_2}^2(\mu_0, \mu_1)$

The term −(K/2)t(1−t)W₂² is the K-convexity correction. When K > 0, entropy is "more convex" than in flat space.

Theorem (Lott-Villani, Sturm)

For a smooth Riemannian manifold (M, g) with volume measure:

CD(K, ∞) holds ⟺ Ric ≥ Kg (Ricci curvature bounded below by K)

This is extraordinary: a purely metric/measure-theoretic condition (convexity of entropy along optimal transport geodesics) captures the differential-geometric notion of Ricci curvature!

Why "synthetic"? This definition makes sense on:

  • Smooth manifolds (recovers classical Ricci)
  • Metric spaces without smooth structure
  • Discrete spaces like graphs
  • Limits of manifolds (Gromov-Hausdorff limits)
  • Sub-Riemannian manifolds

8. Consequences of Curvature Bounds

Ricci curvature bounds have profound implications:

Consequences of Ric ≥ K > 0
  • Bonnet-Myers: Diameter is bounded by π/√K
  • Bishop-Gromov: Volume growth is controlled
  • Poincaré inequality: Spectral gap ≥ K
  • Log-Sobolev: Fast convergence to equilibrium
  • Concentration: Measures concentrate on typical sets

For gradient flows: If Ric ≥ K, the heat equation converges to equilibrium exponentially fast with rate K:

Exponential Convergence
$$\textcolor{#a855f7}{W_2}(\rho_{\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}}, \rho_\infty) \leq e^{-\textcolor{#22c55e}{K}\textcolor{#fbbf24}{t}} \textcolor{#a855f7}{W_2}(\rho_0, \rho_\infty)$$

9. Implications for Machine Learning

This geometric framework directly impacts generative modeling:

Geometric Concept ML Interpretation
W₂ geodesic Optimal interpolation between distributions
Gradient flow Diffusion process / score-based generation
Entropy Negative log-likelihood
Ric ≥ K > 0 Fast convergence, good mixing
Ric < 0 Slow convergence, mode collapse risk

Flow matching learns the velocity field along W₂ geodesics from noise to data. The quality of this approximation depends on the geometry of the data manifold.

Riemannian score matching extends score-based diffusion to data on manifolds (e.g., protein structures on SO(3), shapes on Stiefel manifolds). The score function becomes ∇Mlog ρ—the Riemannian gradient.

The punchline: Understanding the Riemannian structure of Wasserstein space explains why diffusion models work, predicts their convergence properties, and guides the design of better algorithms for non-Euclidean data.

Central Insight

The space of probability distributions is a Riemannian manifold. PDEs are gradient flows. Curvature bounds = entropy convexity. This geometric view unifies optimal transport, PDEs, and generative modeling.
